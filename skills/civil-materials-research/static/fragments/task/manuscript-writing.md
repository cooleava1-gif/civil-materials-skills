# Task: Manuscript Writing

Use when drafting or rebuilding title, abstract, highlights, introduction, results, discussion, conclusion, or cover letter.

For final English polishing or Chinese-to-English journal-style tightening, prefer the companion skill `civil-materials-polishing` after the evidence chain is clear.

## Abstract logic

Use this order:

1. Context: civil engineering problem.
2. Gap: what existing materials/tests do not resolve.
3. Approach: material design and key methods.
4. Results: quantitative findings from supplied data only.
5. Mechanism: evidence-supported explanation.
6. Implication: bounded engineering relevance.

## Introduction logic

Use this funnel:

1. Infrastructure/materials challenge.
2. Current material or modification strategy.
3. Remaining bottleneck in performance, durability, interface, or constructability.
4. Why this study's design variable matters.
5. Objective and evidence plan.

## Results and discussion

Do not write a lab diary. Build evidence ladders:

- Composition or microstructure -> property change -> durability/interface response -> mechanism -> engineering boundary.

Separate Results from Discussion if the target journal expects it. If the user is unsure, use a combined "Results and discussion" structure common in materials journals.

## Negative or inconclusive results

Do not hide non-significant, contradictory, or trade-off results. Write them as boundary-defining evidence:

- Non-significant result: report the trend, p value or uncertainty, and avoid `significant`.
- High-dosage decrease: discuss threshold effects, viscosity, phase separation, or constructability only with supporting evidence.
- Hypothesis mismatch: state what the measurement did not show, then revise the mechanism claim.
- Failed or excluded specimen: explain the valid exclusion reason, such as instrument failure or specimen detachment, not result preference.

Safe sentence:

`Although the 15% epoxy group showed a higher mean pull-off strength, the difference from the 10% group was not statistically significant. This result is treated as a dosage trend rather than definitive evidence for an optimum.`

## English style

- Prefer direct, short sentences.
- Use "may", "suggests", and "is attributed to" when mechanism is inferred.
- Avoid promotional words unless the evidence is strong.
